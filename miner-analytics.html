Sector Context

The Bitcoin mining sector is undergoing its most dramatic transformation since the China mining ban of 2021, with the industry rapidly evolving from pure-play cryptocurrency operations into diversified digital infrastructure companies. The numbers tell the story: over $70 billion in AI and HPC contracts have been signed, and some miners could derive up to 70% of their revenue from AI by year-end 2026.

The economic logic is straightforward but painful. The weighted average cash cost to mine one Bitcoin among public miners has risen to approximately $80,000 in Q4 2025, while BTC trades around $70,000, implying losses of roughly $10,000 per coin mined. This unsustainable math is forcing the pivot: miners are selling Bitcoin reserves, restructuring boards, rebranding companies (Bitfarms to Keel Infrastructure), and signing multi-billion-dollar leases with hyperscalers and AI labs.

Today's market reflects this transition. The Hormuz blockade pushed oil past $102 and Bitcoin below $71,000, creating headwinds for miners on both the energy cost and BTC revenue sides. The Fed's revised 2.7% inflation forecast suggests rate relief won't come quickly, keeping financing costs elevated for capital-intensive data center builds. Yet the contracted revenue pipeline - TeraWulf's $12.8B, Core Scientific's $10.2B, Hut 8's $7B - provides multi-year visibility that pure mining never offered.

The divergence between "HODL miners" (MARA with 53,822 BTC, CleanSpark with 13,513 BTC) and "pivot miners" (IREN holding zero BTC, CORZ selling $175M worth) is widening into a clear strategic split. Investors are being forced to choose between a Bitcoin treasury thesis and an infrastructure-as-a-service thesis, with few companies successfully straddling both. The next 90 days will be critical as Q1 earnings reveal whether the HPC revenue ramp is matching the contracted pipeline's promise.
